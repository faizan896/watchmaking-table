Feuille I · Histoire1859 — aujourd'hui

We come from the people
who measured the country.

In 1859, Édouard Calame was not a watchmaker. He was an instrument maker — ruling pens, beam compasses, self-compensating dividers — supplying the engineers of the Dufour survey, the men who triangulated the Alps to produce the first accurate map of Switzerland.

  • 1859Fondation

    A workshop in Le Locle, seated between the watchmakers and the cartographers. Calame sold to both. Every line he ruled meant something; the habit never left the house.

  • 1874Les onze

    An engineer returning from a season on the Finsteraarhorn asks for an observatory movement cased in something he can wear on a cord — legible in fog, adjustable with gloves, honest about its error. Calame builds eleven. The watches come almost reluctantly.

  • 1874–1971Service

    Chronometers for surveyors, railway engineers, and the federal observatory at Neuchâtel. Each instrument ships with its individual rate certificate, deviations printed plainly.

  • 1971Fermeture

    Quartz ends it quietly. No drama, no fire sale. The tooling goes into crates.

  • 2019Réouverture

    The crates are opened by three people: a great-great-granddaughter who spent her career at Swisstopo drawing contour lines by hand, a movement constructor who left a large maison out of boredom, and an enamelist from Geneva.

  • Aujourd'huiCapacité

    Around six hundred watches a year, in the original building. There is no intention to make more.

Feuille II · CartesLégende des signes

The dial is a sheet,
not a face.

A map tells you where you are in the land; a watch tells you where you are in the day. Everything on a Calame dial must serve orientation — nothing may serve display. If a detail cannot justify itself the way a line on a survey sheet must, it is deleted.

Légende — read as on a Siegfried sheet

  • Le méridienRecessed hairline · 12 → 6

    A single recessed line runs vertically through the dial. It costs almost nothing to notice; once seen, it cannot be unseen. No one else has it.

  • Courbes de niveauContour relief · 0.04 mm

    Not guilloché. Contour lines milled four hundredths of a millimetre deep — invisible head-on, appearing only when the wrist tilts, the way relief appears on a map when light rakes across it.

  • Aiguilles-compasDivider needles

    Long, tapered, counterweighted like a compass needle. The seconds tip is lacquered in benchmark vermilion — the one red allowed on the sheet, used exactly once.

  • Couronne micromètreKnurled crown

    Knurled like a micrometer drum — fine, functional, a gloved-hand grip. Lugs are drawn with a single French curve; no facets, no crab claws.

  • L'heure vraieSignature complication

    A small subsidiary hand showing local solar noon for the owner's set longitude — the surveyor's time against civil time. Quietly useful, historically grounded, owned by no one else. It is our only signature complication, and we consider that plenty.

  • Point d'origineCaseback coordinates

    Casebacks carry the coordinates of the workshop; each serial number is expressed as an offset from that point. Every watch knows where it comes from.

Typography follows the survey plates: the wordmark in the engraver's sloped italic used for rivers and summits — printed, never applied, for applied logos are jewelry — and numerals in the narrow upright figures used for towns, with their slightly flat-topped 4 and open 6. We do not use Helvetica. Being Swiss is not a font.

Feuille III · AtelierLe Locle · 946 m

The original building,
at actual capacity.

Three people opened the crates in 2019. A cartographer who drew contour lines by hand at Swisstopo. A movement constructor who left a large maison out of boredom. An enamelist from Geneva, whose kiln now stands where the dividing engine stood.

Six hundred pieces a year is not scarcity theater — it is the actual capacity of the building. The workshop sits where it always has, between the watchmakers and the cartographers, and it still sells to the discipline of both.

All documentation of the atelier is made as record, not as advertisement: raking light, measured scale, numbered figures. Nothing is staged, because nothing needs to be.

There is a seat free at the bench. The calibre O-46 is laid out in its trays — plate, train, bridges, balance — and the lamp is on. Assemble it yourself → L'Établi

Feuille IV · MouvementCalibre O-46 · maillechort

Honest about error.

Our chronometers shipped with their individual rate certificates, deviations printed plainly. We still publish each watch's observed rate rather than a marketing tolerance. Patience over spectacle: we build no complication whose only purpose is to be difficult.

Movements are made in untreated maillechort — nickel silver — which slowly takes on a warm patina. The watch ages the way a survey instrument does: honestly, and in proportion to its use. It is visible through the caseback, beside the coordinates of the workshop.

Feuille V · MatièresRien d'enduit, rien qui prétend

Territory dictates
the palette of matter.

  • Acier & tantale

    Cases in hand-finished steel and tantalum — the blue-grey of glacial water, heavy, stubborn to machine, indifferent to fashion.

  • Argent oxydé · grand feu

    Dials in oxidized solid silver, or grand feu enamel from our own kiln.

  • Maillechort

    Untreated nickel silver for the movement, patinating in the open. Nothing coated, nothing pretending.

  • Cuir tanné végétal

    Straps from a Bernese tannery, stitched flat. No gold as a default, no diamonds ever, no ceramic, no “forged carbon.”

Feuille VII · Commission4 274 m — sommet de l'atlas

Commission your instrument.

A commission is opened as correspondence, not as a transaction. Tell us who you are and where you live in the world; the second question is not curiosity — the heure vraie must be regulated to your longitude before the movement is cased.

At 6°44′57″ E, local solar noon precedes civil noon by roughly 33 minutes in winter. Your instrument will know this.

Open correspondence

We answer in the order received, and we answer slowly. If a season passes, the season was needed.
